## 1. Defining the Hard Problem

### 1.1 Easy vs Hard Problems

**Easy Problems (Solvable in principle):**
- How does the brain process information?
- How does perception work?
- How does memory form?
- How does attention function?

**These are "easy" because they just require explaining MECHANISMS.**

**The Hard Problem:**
> Why is there subjective experience AT ALL?
> Why doesn't all this processing happen "in the dark"?

### 1.2 The Explanatory Gap

```
WHAT WE CAN EXPLAIN:
====================
Neurons fire -> Signals propagate -> Brain processes

WHAT WE CANNOT EXPLAIN:
=======================
...-> ??? -> Subjective experience (the "redness" of red)

The ??? is the Hard Problem.
```

### 1.3 Why It's Hard

**No matter how much brain function we describe:**
- Chemical reactions
- Electrical signals
- Neural networks
- Information processing

**We cannot deduce that there should be EXPERIENCE.**

## 2. Failed Solutions

### 2.1 Eliminativism

**Claim:** Consciousness doesn't really exist. It's an illusion.

**Problem:** Who/what is having the illusion? The explanation requires what it denies.

### 2.2 Functionalism

**Claim:** Consciousness IS the function. Nothing more to explain.

**Problem:** Why do functions need to feel like something? A thermostat functions without experience.

### 2.3 Panpsychism

**Claim:** Consciousness is in all matter. Particles have tiny experiences that combine.

**Problem:** The "combination problem" ‚Äî how do little experiences combine into unified consciousness?

### 2.4 Emergentism

**Claim:** Consciousness "emerges" from complexity.

**Problem:** Emergence explains new PROPERTIES, not new ONTOLOGIES. How does non-experience become experience?
