# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 The Measurement Problem

Quantum mechanics is the most successful physical theory ever devised, yet it contains a fundamental mystery: **why does observation change reality?**

In classical physics, measurement reveals pre-existing properties. A thermometer doesn't create temperature — it reads it. But in quantum mechanics, particles exist in superposition (multiple states simultaneously) until observed. Upon observation, the wave function "collapses" into a single definite state.

```
BEFORE OBSERVATION:          AFTER OBSERVATION:
|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩            |0⟩ or |1⟩
(Superposition)              (Definite state)

QUESTION:
What constitutes an "observation"?
What causes the collapse?
```

This is the **measurement problem** — physics' greatest unsolved puzzle.

## 1.2 Historical Context

### 1.2.1 The Copenhagen Interpretation (1920s)

Bohr and Heisenberg proposed that:
- The wave function represents our knowledge, not reality itself
- Observation "creates" the outcome
- The observer is essential but undefined

### 1.2.2 The Many-Worlds Interpretation (1957)

Everett proposed:
- No collapse occurs — all outcomes happen in branching universes
- The observer is not special
- Problem: No explanation of why we experience only one branch

### 1.2.3 Decoherence (1970s-present)

Environment-induced decoherence explains:
- How interference patterns disappear
- How classical behavior emerges
- BUT: Doesn't explain why one outcome is experienced

### 1.2.4 Current State

After 100 years, no consensus exists. The observer remains undefined.
