### Research Question

Why does 95% of the universe appear "invisible" despite affecting spacetime measurably?

### Background

Since Fritz Zwicky's 1933 observation of the Coma cluster, physics has acknowledged that visible matter cannot explain galactic dynamics. The rotation curves of galaxies (Vera Rubin, 1970s), gravitational lensing, and cosmic microwave background measurements all indicate massive amounts of "dark matter" — matter that gravitates but doesn't emit or absorb light. Additionally, the 1998 discovery that cosmic expansion is accelerating revealed "dark energy" — an unknown force driving space apart. Together, these invisible components constitute ~95% of the universe's mass-energy content.

After 90 years, no dark matter particle has been detected despite extensive searches (XENON, LUX, PandaX, DAMA, LHC). This paper proposes an alternative framework: dark matter and dark energy are not mysterious new particles or fields, but normal matter and energy existing in adjacent "frequency bands" or Lokas — rendering layers that affect our spacetime while remaining invisible to our senses and instruments.
